Volumetric Display Log — Krupal

This is where I'm documenting LED display experiments. No structure, just thoughts and progress as it happens.

The following is my way through understanding and trying to make a volumetric display. Some parts of it are flagged nonchalant, they contain what is irrelevant to the volumetric thing.

The Journey Begins [>]

It started with Voxon Photonics happening to my YT feed. It felt magical to me and a constant urge to make one for myself, I just want to hold it in my hands, and see with my naked eyes. But I was busy researching in maths (Ramsey's theorem) and distro hopping.

Then mitxela happened to my feed. With a candle. A what? A candle on a tiny rotating LED matrix which creates a POV (persistence of vision - your eyes see motion as continuous) to fake 3D. His setup: 8x10 LED matrix, a small DC motor, Li coin cell battery, rotate it fast enough, refresh the matrix proportionally fast enough and boom, you see 3D!

The ultimatum was James Brown's YT video. He dropped a banger, assembly video of this vortex, the fucking 128x64 LED matrix volumetric display. This guy plays doom on this! I couldn't wait much. I started building one.

7th December, I ordered: - 8x8 generic LED matrix with MAX7219 driver (chip that controls multiple LEDs) - ESP8266 (WiFi microcontroller) - BLDC motor (brushless DC motor - more precise than regular motors) - Some jumper wires

Labour leverage (Naval style), made GPT build an iterative LED matrix control board webpage for me.

Nothing worked, no LEDs on. Was thinking I got a wrong module, after scratching my ass on this for like two evenings, I realised the anodes and cathodes were interchanged. Lolol it light up and boom, I was now a hardware guy, I told to myself. (I confirm I'm still not one)

Bought a 12V 2A power supply and started with BLDC, this shit won't calibrate for real. Bought an IR sensor (detects rotation) and again GPT for helping me measure the RPM of this motor. Had much fun with this!

But was running low with budget. A friend suggested bagel fund, I applied got $250.

Building Prototypes [*]

Started designing my first prototype. My v0 was named kirmaadaa, a friend suggested and I kept it. No James Brown inspiration, only mitxela and my own tinkering. I love Blender (3D modeling software), designed one, got it 3D printed, and it was rotating like crazy! I was thinking to transfer power wirelessly. Very scrappy design but gave it a go.

v0 rotating video

Problems: Vibrations were crazy, design was not parametric (couldn't adjust dimensions easily).

V1 coming in! Learned CAD (computer aided design), Fusion 360. Implemented a timing belt system, two ESP32 (upgraded microcontrollers) instead of one. ESPNow communication (wireless communication protocol) between them. PCB designing (printed circuit board - custom electronic boards), Easy EDA. Watched the vortex assembly video for around 60-80 times.

Pavitra, building rockets back then and planes now, helped me with mechanical design. Mantra, an EC senior, helped with electronics a bit. Still code was a weak area for me.

The electronics did not change much: - Custom PCB for driver and LED - Modules for IR and encoder (position sensor) for RPM control

Main highlight: custom slip ring implementation (device that allows power transfer while rotating). Seeing it spin at 1800 RPM and transferring 5V 5A current was gonna be cool!

I fucked up at choosing LEDs and LED drivers, both. I had the PCBs in my hand, and the guilt to take power electronics for granted, a regret.

Learning & Understanding [+]

I paused this project. EV grant came in, learned more power electronics and general electronics from basics: - Diodes, gates, timer IC - PWM (pulse width modulation - controlling power) - SPI/I2C (communication protocols) - Buck-boost (voltage converters) - AC-DC converters - Some low level C programming

Most of my time went understanding what James Brown did, from his design to the code. Devin helped a lot in going through the git repo (code repository).

So the code has three main parts:

  1. Vortex - runs on RPI (Raspberry Pi - small computer) and lights up the actual matrix
  2. Virtex - OpenGL thing (graphics library), which simulates the actual display
  3. Viewer - helps import 3D objects and images to memory buffer which is read by virtex and vortex

I learned OpenGL and much of computer graphics while going through the repo.
